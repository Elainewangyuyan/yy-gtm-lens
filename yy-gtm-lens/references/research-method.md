# Research method

## Evidence hierarchy

Prefer sources in this order:

1. Regulatory filings, company disclosures, official product/pricing/careers pages.
2. Official papers, model cards, repositories and benchmark code.
3. Founder or executive interviews with date and venue.
4. Reputable reporting with named documents or multiple corroborating sources.
5. Independent analytics and evaluations with methodology.
6. Social posts, job aggregators and anonymous claims.

Use lower-tier evidence only with explicit qualifiers.

## Temporal accuracy

- State the research cutoff using an exact date.
- Recheck current leadership, pricing, products, funding status, hiring and model releases.
- Distinguish event date from publication date.
- Add access dates to dynamic pages.
- Never treat a historical metric as current.

## Claim labels

- **Fact:** directly supported by primary material.
- **Company-reported:** stated by the company but not independently verified.
- **Media disclosure:** reported by a reputable outlet, usually via sources.
- **Independent evaluation:** third-party test with a disclosed method.
- **Inference:** analyst interpretation based on cited evidence.
- **Unknown:** important but not publicly verifiable.

## High-risk facts requiring direct citations

- Revenue, ARR, growth rate, MAU/DAU, paid users, retention and margins.
- Funding amount, completed valuation, proposed valuation and cap table.
- Investor/shareholder ownership.
- Customer status and contract size.
- Model architecture, training tokens, benchmark scores and license.
- Headcount, leadership, reporting lines and open roles.
- Legal, safety, privacy and governance disputes.

## Common traps

- Customer logo ≠ paying enterprise customer.
- Integration ≠ revenue.
- GitHub stars/downloads ≠ active developers.
- Relative growth ≠ meaningful scale.
- Open weights ≠ unrestricted open source.
- Verify the repository files and license on the cutoff date. If the product page and repository disagree, report the repository state and note the documentation lag.
- For funding, shareholders and founder control, prefer official prospectuses and filings. Do not use Wikipedia as the decisive source.
- For lawsuits or misconduct allegations, separate each case, name the claimant and date, and label the procedural status.
- Mark pricing, repository activity, job listings and market value as dynamic facts with an accessed date.
- Model benchmark ≠ product reliability or ROI.
- Careers entry ≠ verified current role list.
- High valuation ≠ capital efficiency.

## GTM analysis

Connect recommendations to a measurable bottleneck:

- Awareness → qualified traffic.
- Activation → first successful high-value task.
- Retention → repeated workflow use.
- Monetization → subscription/API/enterprise conversion.
- Expansion → seats, usage, teams and production deployment.

Prefer a prioritized 0–90 day plan over a long unranked idea list.

For each recommendation specify:

- Target audience or workflow.
- Funnel bottleneck.
- Proposed experiment.
- Required asset or channel.
- Success metric.
- Review window.
- Why the experiment is low-cost relative to alternatives.

## Career and reach-out analysis

Keep this material private. Separate:

- Whether the company is worth contacting.
- Whether the specific role is worth joining.
- Whether the reporting line, authority, data access and compensation support success.

Validate role scope, manager, budget, product influence, commercial data, equity terms and organizational stability before recommending that the user join.
